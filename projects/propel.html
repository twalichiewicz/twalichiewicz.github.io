Smoothing food stamp spending

Reframing to stop windfall.

A little bit about food stamps

The Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) supports a significant portion of the US population, with over 45.8 million individuals benefiting from it, roughly equivalent to 1 in 7 Americans. This program plays a vital role in the United States, as it is estimated that 50% of Americans will become eligible for SNAP at some point during their lives. Its purpose is to provide assistance to low-income families by offering an average monthly food allowance of $250 per household.

However, the current method of administering benefits on a once-per-month basis has unintended negative consequences on behavior, often referred to as the "windfall" effect. People tend to anchor their spending decisions on the balance they have at the beginning of the month, leading them to disregard future expenses. This effect is particularly pronounced when individuals have experienced periods of scarcity, as the sudden deposit of a monthly sum often results in overspending and misallocation of funds.

Interestingly, this phenomenon affects both lottery winners and food stamp recipients in similar ways, the amount received regardless of the actual amount they receive. Regrettably, this mindset adversely impacts the very people SNAP aims to assist. According to the USDA, many families exhaust their SNAP benefits before the end of the month, highlighting the urgency to address the issue.

Let’s fix this

Remember that "windfall" effect I talked about earlier? Well, we already knew that the USDA has reported that SNAP recipients have a hard time smoothing (spending a balance as close to 1:1 as possible) their spending during the month. So I wondered if we could fix this long-standing problem by adjusting the way that people learn what their balance is.
